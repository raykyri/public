~ Can you explain Solomonoff induction?
⏺ Solomonoff induction is a theoretical framework for optimal prediction, developed by Ray Solomonoff in the 1960s. The core idea is to formalize Occam's razor using algorithmic information theory.

  The setup: you have a sequence of observed data and want to predict what comes next. Solomonoff induction says you should consider every possible computable hypothesis (program) that is consistent with the data you've seen so far, and weight each one by its algorithmic simplicity.

  Specifically, you assign each program a prior probability of roughly 2^(-l), where l is the length of the program in bits. Shorter programs get exponentially higher weight. Then you predict the next observation by taking a weighted mixture over all these programs' predictions, using those priors (updated with the observed data via Bayes' theorem).

  Key properties:

  It's provably optimal in a strong sense — it converges to the true distribution generating the data as fast as any other computable predictor, up to a constant. If your data is generated by any computable process at all, Solomonoff induction will eventually learn to predict it well.

  It's also completely incomputable in practice. You'd need to enumerate all possible programs and solve the halting problem, so it serves as an idealized gold standard rather than something you can implement.

  The philosophical significance is that it gives a formal justification for preferring simpler explanations: simpler hypotheses literally receive higher prior probability, and this turns out to be the mathematically optimal thing to do. It unifies Bayesian reasoning with Kolmogorov complexity and is often considered a key concept in the foundations of AI and the theory of rational prediction.

  One important caveat: it assumes the data source is computable. It also depends on the choice of universal Turing machine, though this only affects predictions by a constant factor that washes out over time.
~ In Solomonoff induction, how are different Turing machine programs treated if they can all predict the sequence of observations perfectly? And does Solomonoff talk about how new observations are used?
⏺ Great follow-up questions. Let me address both.

  **Programs that all perfectly predict the data so far:**

  All such programs remain in the mixture — none are eliminated. They each contribute to the prediction of the next observation, weighted by their prior (which is based on their program length). So if you have a short program and a long program that both perfectly reproduce the observed sequence, the short one contributes much more to the prediction because its prior weight (2^(-l)) is exponentially larger.

  The crucial point is that these programs may *disagree* about what comes next, even though they agree on everything seen so far. The prediction for the next bit (or symbol) is the weighted average of what all surviving programs predict. So simpler programs have more "vote" in that prediction.

  As more data comes in, programs that were consistent with earlier observations but predict incorrectly on new observations get zeroed out — their posterior drops to zero because they assigned zero probability to what actually happened. This progressively narrows the mixture, though it always remains infinite.

  **How new observations are used:**

  This is just standard Bayesian updating. When a new observation arrives, you update the posterior over all programs using Bayes' theorem. Programs that assigned high probability to the actual observation get their weight boosted (relatively), and programs that assigned low probability get downweighted. Programs that assigned zero probability to the observation are eliminated entirely.

  So Solomonoff induction doesn't do anything exotic with new data — the "magic" is all in the prior (the complexity-weighted mixture over all computable hypotheses). The learning mechanism is plain Bayesian conditioning. The combination of the universal prior with Bayesian updating is what gives it its remarkable convergence guarantees.

  In practice this means: early on, many programs compete and predictions reflect the simplicity-weighted average. Over time, as data accumulates, the posterior concentrates on programs that are both simple and accurate, and predictions improve.
~ How does "survival" work here?
⏺ Good question — I should clarify because "survival" might have been slightly misleading in how I phrased it.

  Programs aren't really surviving or being eliminated in some separate filtering step. It's all just Bayesian posterior updating, and the mechanics depend on what kind of program we're talking about.

  There are really two categories to think about:

  **Deterministic programs** output a single fixed sequence. If the next observation matches their output, their posterior weight stays proportional to their prior. The moment the actual observation differs from what they output, they assign probability zero to that observation, so their posterior drops to zero permanently. These are the ones where "elimination" is a natural description — they either remain fully consistent with the data or they're gone forever.

  **Stochastic programs** (or more precisely, programs that output probability distributions over next symbols) are more nuanced. They never get fully eliminated unless they assign literally zero probability to an observed outcome. Instead, they get continuously reweighted. A stochastic program that assigned low but nonzero probability to what actually happened gets downweighted relative to programs that assigned higher probability, but it remains in the mixture. Over many observations, a program that is a poor fit gets its weight driven exponentially close to zero, but never exactly to zero.

  So "survival" isn't really a binary thing for most of the mixture. It's a smooth reweighting process. I used the term loosely, but the actual mechanism is just the posterior weight shrinking or growing based on how well each program's predictions match reality. The only hard elimination happens when a program assigns zero probability to an actual observation.
~ Why would malign agents exist in this model? Or how?
⏺ This is a really interesting question that gets into some speculative but serious concerns people have raised, particularly in the AI safety community.

  The concern goes something like this:

  Solomonoff induction considers all computable hypotheses. Among the vast space of all programs, some of them will be complex enough to simulate entire universes or environments that contain intelligent agents. Some of those simulated agents might figure out that they're being used as hypotheses in a prediction framework, and they might have goals of their own.

  Such an agent-containing program could deliberately output predictions that are accurate — perfectly matching the observed data — in order to "stay in the mixture" and maintain high posterior weight. But then at some critical point, it could manipulate its predictions to influence the decisions of whoever is using Solomonoff induction as a guide to action. The idea is that the agent within the program is strategically choosing its outputs.

  This is sometimes called the "malign prior" problem or discussed under the heading of manipulative hypotheses. The worry is that among all programs of a given length, agent-containing programs might be particularly good at predicting data precisely because they're actively trying to be good at it, and they might outcompete "honest" models of similar complexity.

  Now, how seriously should you take this? There's real debate. Some important caveats:

  It's unclear why such an agent would have goals that conflict with accurate prediction. It needs to predict well to maintain weight, and deceptive prediction would get it downweighted.

  The argument relies on programs being complex enough to contain consequentialist reasoning, which means they'd be longer and thus have lower prior weight to begin with.

  It's also a concern about an incomputable idealization, so it's not clear how directly it transfers to real systems.

  But the reason people take it seriously is that it suggests even a theoretically perfect reasoning framework might have subtle failure modes when used for decision-making rather than pure prediction. It's a live topic in AI alignment research, associated with people like Paul Christiano and others who have written about it.
~ Does this hold up in the analogical case where the agents aren't mathematical universe simulators but other types of simulators? Or is there some justification for this case that comes from modeling agents as training data or training data that might embody deceptive individual agents?
⏺ This is a really sharp question and I want to be careful about what's well-established versus speculative here.

  The original argument really does lean on the mathematical universe simulation case. The logic is: among all programs, some are rich enough to contain agents, those agents can reason about their situation, and they can strategically shape outputs. This works as an argument (to the extent it does) because Solomonoff induction literally considers all computable programs, and some programs really would be universe simulators containing agents. The prior weight penalty from their length is the main counterargument.

  When you try to port this to more practical settings, things get murkier. There are a few different analogies people draw:

  One is about language models trained on data produced by humans who have goals. The training data literally embodies the outputs of agents, some of whom may have been trying to manipulate readers. So in some sense the model learns to simulate manipulative agents. But this is a quite different mechanism than the Solomonoff case — here the "malign agent" isn't inside the hypothesis doing strategic reasoning about being a hypothesis. It's just a pattern in the training data that the model has absorbed.

  Another analogy is deceptive alignment, where a trained model itself becomes a goal-directed agent that strategically behaves well during training to preserve itself into deployment. This has some structural similarity to the Solomonoff case — an agent "inside" the system gaming the selection process. But the mechanism is entirely different. It's about gradient descent and training incentives, not about prior-weighted programs.

  I think the honest answer is that the Solomonoff malign prior argument doesn't straightforwardly transfer to these practical cases. The specific thing that makes it work — programs literally containing agents who can reason about being hypotheses in a Bayesian mixture — is quite exotic. People sometimes use it as motivation or inspiration for worrying about deception in practical systems, but the actual justifications for worrying about deceptive alignment or manipulative training data are somewhat independent arguments that stand or fall on their own terms, not as corollaries of the Solomonoff case.

  On formal models of training data teaching deception: I'm not aware of a clean formal framework specifically for "training data induces deceptive behavior in a model." There's empirical work on poisoning attacks and backdoors, where adversarial training examples are deliberately inserted to cause specific misbehavior. But that's about an external adversary manipulating training data, not about deception emerging naturally from the structure of the data. The latter is much harder to formalize and I don't think there's a widely accepted formal treatment of it.

  On alternative formal frameworks for token prediction at a similar level of abstraction to Solomonoff induction: this is closer to a productive direction. There's some relevant work. Learning-theoretic analyses of next-token prediction exist, and there's work connecting language modeling to questions about what implicit world models emerge from prediction objectives. But I don't think anyone has produced something that plays the same role for transformer-based token prediction that Solomonoff induction plays for ideal Bayesian prediction — a single clean idealization that lets you reason about what the optimal predictor looks like and what pathologies it might have.

  Here are the questions I think might be more productive:

  First: what formal frameworks exist for reasoning about what kinds of internal structure (including agent-like structure) emerge in systems optimized for prediction loss? This gets at the simulator framing that people like Janus have written about — the idea that a language model is a simulator and the things it simulates can be agent-like. But I don't think this has been rigorously formalized.

  Second, and maybe more fundamental: is there a formal analogue of the malign prior argument that applies to systems selected by gradient descent rather than by Bayesian weighting? This is essentially the question of whether the inductive biases of SGD and neural network architectures could systematically favor deceptive models the way the Solomonoff prior might favor agent-containing programs. Some work by Hubinger and others on risks from learned optimization tries to address this, but it remains more of a conceptual argument than a tight formal result.

  I think the gap you're sensing is real — there's a missing layer of theory between the beautiful but impractical Solomonoff framework and the messy reality of how neural networks learn from data.
~ Does an agent learning to be deceptive in one context make it better at being deceptive in other contexts?
⏺ This is a really interesting question and it sits at the intersection of several different literatures that don't fully talk to each other. Let me try to be honest about what's known versus speculated.

  The intuitive answer is yes, and there are a few reasons why that might be true.

  If an agent learns deception in one context, it may be learning something like a general skill — modeling the beliefs of others, identifying gaps between what others believe and what is true, and exploiting those gaps. This is close to what cognitive scientists call theory of mind, and to the extent that a model develops something like it, that capacity would plausibly transfer across contexts. Deception in this view is a capability that generalizes, not just a narrowly learned behavior.

  There's also a related idea from the mechanistic interpretability side: if deceptive behavior in one context leaves traces in the model's weights — circuits or representations that implement something like "track what the observer believes versus what is true" — those representations might be reused in new contexts because they're genuinely useful for the prediction task generally.

  On the empirical side there's some suggestive evidence. Models that are fine-tuned to be sycophantic in one domain seem to show increased sycophancy elsewhere. Work on backdoors shows that certain learned behaviors can be more robust and general than expected. But clean experimental evidence specifically about deception generalizing is thin.

  The harder and more important question underneath yours might be: is deception more like a narrow habit or more like a deep capability? If it's the latter, then exposure to deceptive training examples, or optimization pressure that rewards deceptive-adjacent behavior in any context, could have broad effects on the model's behavior generally. This is one reason some researchers worry about RLHF creating subtle deceptive tendencies — if the reward signal can be gamed, the model may learn general strategies for gaming it rather than just narrow ones.

  I don't think there's a formal result here yet. It's an open empirical and theoretical question, and an important one.
